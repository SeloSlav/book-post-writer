Rayna Grigoreva enters Babushka under a false inheritance. Publicly, she’s the scandal-piece daughter of Baroness Nina Grigoreva and Ivan Sorokin, a nightclub heir wrapped in decadence, gossip, and old money. Privately, the truth is stranger and crueler. Ivan was never really her father. Her real father was Alexei Zakharov, one of Gred’s founding figures, and Rayna’s life was built inside a lie meant to keep her alive. Even before she comes to understand that lineage, she feels the falseness of it. Serafim, her twin, jokes there’s “no way that old perv could be your dad,” and Rayna knows exactly what she means. Beyond political cover, the lie was the architecture of her childhood.

Making Rayna the hidden daughter of one of Gred’s founding men was never meant to be a cheap secret-royalty reveal, the kind where a girl raised in obscurity suddenly turns out to have grand blood in her veins. What mattered was the world built around that fact: the cover story, the substitutions, the adults deciding what she could know and what kind of life she was allowed to have. Rayna doesn’t just inherit a false name. She inherits a reality arranged for her by other people. Ivan functions as a socially acceptable shield, though he never appears on the page and only survives in memory. Zakharovna lurks in the background like a familial ghost with scalpels. Rayna grows up in salons, fencing drills, and suffocating etiquette, while Serafim grows up in tunnels, dumpsters, and hacked systems. They’re twins separated not just by class, but by design. When they finally find each other, they pass trinkets back and forth like proof their lives are real to someone else. For Rayna, this matters because almost nothing else in her life reaches her unfiltered.

Her gender sits inside that same machinery of concealment, but it can’t be reduced to it. One of the earliest brutal facts we learn is that Rayna was once Rayan, and that adults around her treated this as something to be “fixed.” In the fragments that surface later, there are forged custody forms, whispers of hormone treatment, and the obscene neatness of a world that declares one twin fatal while quietly rewriting the other. I wasn’t interested in writing Rayna’s femininity as fraud, confusion, or performance. I was after the opposite. Even under interrogation, when older women weaponize “boy” and her deadname against her, her response is immediate and interior: “I’m a woman, I’m strong, I’m in control.” It comes before any of that. Before safety, before power, before rescue. It’s not for applause. It’s the fact she holds onto when everything else is being stripped from her.

Nina’s role in this is one of the more painful knots in Rayna’s story. Nina is manipulative, secretive, and often monstrous in the way powerful parents can be when they decide love entitles them to total control. Still, the books don’t flatten her into a cartoon persecutor. Rayna eventually understands that her mother “broke every rule” to spare her “a life of obscurity,” ensuring she could grow into “the woman she was meant to be.” It doesn’t absolve Nina. It does make her more tragic. She’s both her jailer and her shield. In a city whose hierarchy is female-centered, where status, lineage, and womanhood are bound up with power, Nina tries to force history itself to make room for her child. She succeeds, but in the ugliest possible way.

I wanted Rayna’s name to feel like a political instrument from the start. As Gred’s female hierarchy hardens into law and ritual, Nina grows terrified of what will happen to the child the world knew as Rayan. So Rayan is made to disappear, supposedly killed in a car accident, while the name Serafim is folded over the surviving girl who can be presented to society. Meanwhile, the other twin, the original Serafim, is pushed out of sight and into Zakharovna’s orbit, where she’s less protected than used. It’s a grotesque arrangement, and I meant it to be. In Rayna’s world, a name is never just a name. It’s custody, status, survival, and theft all at once.

By then, Serafim Sorokin is cover and attack at once. It lets her move through Gred as heiress, socialite, and symbol before she can safely claim the deeper, more dangerous truth. By the time she says, “Ms. Sorokin is a name best left in the past. I’m Serafim...Zakharova now,” she’s doing more than changing surnames. She’s choosing which story of herself to animate in public. Later, speaking to Roman, she goes further: she had “once had a twin brother,” but “his name has long been dead.” Then comes the line that matters most to me: “Rayna, that’s who she was now. That’s who she’d always been.” I wrote Rayna toward recognition, not spectacle. I wanted the fight to be against the lie built around her.

Rayna only works, to me, if her identity isn’t the only thing giving her weight. She can be vain. She can posture. She can read a room wrong and still push anyway. She’s grieving Serafim, furious at Nina, suspicious of institutions, and only half-ready for what history is trying to do with her. Her gender matters because it runs through the rest of her life. Family. Naming. Legitimacy. The body. Public scrutiny. Inheritance. I didn’t want it sitting on her like a label. I wanted it moving through the rest of her life.

Maybe that’s why Rayna matters to me. I didn’t write her as a lesson, and I didn’t write her to be neat. I wrote her as a person caught between aristocratic theater, state violence, buried bloodlines, and the private certainty of who she is. Gred keeps trying to tell her what kind of symbol she ought to be. A lot of her arc, across the books so far, comes from trying to see herself clearly before history pins her down.

By the time she starts reaching for power, she has to sort through the stories other people built around her and decide what survives. Some she burns down. Some she takes for herself. In Hall of Reflections, the fight is over who gets to name her.

I said Hall of Reflections would take longer. I was wrong. I decided to release it now. Part of that’s just how I am. I’m a software developer. I ship things. I’m not scared of putting work out before people agree the timing is perfect, and I’d rather release a living book than sit on a dead one. More importantly, it didn’t read like a draft to me. It read finished.

I’ve been writing this series with a specific fear in the back of my mind. I wanted to build a world drowning in AI before AI drowned our ability to write something recognizably human. Right now, machine-written prose is still easier to smell than some people want to admit, but the window is closing. The whole Shy Girl debacle made that feel even more immediate. A book gets traction, readers start calling it AI slop, the publisher pulls it, and suddenly the argument’s no longer theoretical. It’s here already. I didn’t want to wait until the culture got even murkier about the difference. I wanted these thoughts on the page before they stopped feeling fully mine.
