A lot of readers have told me some version of the same thing about the DATA ANGELS material in Echoes of Immortality.

They loved it. They were hooked by it. They found the whole vibe intoxicating. The encrypted channels, the hard drives, Maria and Dunya slipping through systems they barely control, Serafim showing up with that strange mix of poise, grime, and menace. It felt fun. It felt dangerous. It felt like a slightly cheap spy thriller filtered through Gred’s frozen circuitry. They were also confused.

Now, I hate fake complexity, the kind where a writer withholds basic coherence and then expects applause because nobody can tell what the hell happened. I wasn’t after that with the DATA ANGELS arc.

I wanted something more specific. I wanted the reader to feel the same unstable ground Dunya’s standing on. She’s a hacker, yes. She understands systems. She’s quick. She’s sharper than almost everyone around her. But she’s not omniscient, and she’s not moving through those early chapters with a clean map. She’s entering a zone full of damaged signals, half-truths, compromised people, old betrayals, nested loyalties, and tech whose practical use has long since fused with memory, ideology, family history, and private damage. She knows enough to keep moving. She doesn’t know enough to interpret everything correctly.

I wanted that whole early thread to feel like a puzzle box that had already been opened and re-closed by three different women before Dunya ever touched it. By the time she gets there, nobody’s handing her a neat mission brief. Maria knows some things and not others. Serafim knows some things and refuses to explain them cleanly. Xenia Fedorova had already buried parts of the route in older systems long before Dunya ever met her. Zakharovna’s influence is all over everything, including people who think they escaped her. So when readers feel like the DATA ANGELS material in Book One is exciting but slippery, I think that’s built into the way the whole thing is told. Serafim is probably the single biggest reason it feels that way.

She enters Echoes with what I’d call a cheap spy vibe. She’s got aliases, covert channels, dead drops, scrambled motives, the half-sardonic voice, the look of someone who’s been sleeping in ducts and living off bad coffee and stolen time. At first she feels like the kind of character a weaker version of this series would use as a stock operator, the shady one, the backchannel girl, the one who knows more than she says and always arrives a little too late with blood on her sleeve and some cryptic line about layers. I knew exactly what I was doing there because I wanted readers to half-enjoy her and half-distrust her.

Serafim throws Maria off almost right away. In the Oblivion Spire sections, Maria keeps feeling that Serafim is moving through a different version of the mission than the one she’s willing to name. When Maria asks what she saw in the mirror levels, Serafim gives her that cold answer about seeing “what I needed to see,” and Maria feels the dodge for what it is. You can feel the trust thinning there.

The DATA ANGELS material moves with a pulpy kind of speed in Book One. She’s still early enough in the story that things can look simpler than they really are. The hacker plot, the missing partner, the suspicious intermediary, the secret drive, the compromised sim, the mysterious contact, the dead woman who maybe knew too much, the backdoor into the larger system. But because this is Babushka, none of it stays that neat for long. To me, the interesting thing is that it starts off looking like a cyberpunk side plot and ends up tied into some of the deepest family and emotional stuff in the whole series. That’s where Serafim matters.

A lot of readers, especially after Book One, come away thinking Serafim is basically a backstabber with a tragic edge. Someone who maybe helps Dunya, maybe manipulates her, maybe uses Maria, maybe pursues her own anti-Zakharovna agenda while treating everyone around her like disposable parts. I get why people read her that way. Book One encourages it. She withholds constantly. She pushes Dunya without soothing her. She does things that, from Dunya’s point of view, absolutely feel like betrayal. She makes herself look like a hostile actor at exactly the moments when trust would matter most. All of that is deliberate, but what I never wanted was a flat backstabbing story.

Serafim’s not there to provide a cheap twist where the shady one turns out to be shady. That bores me. What interested me was something meaner and more human. I wanted a character who helps in ways that are almost indistinguishable from sabotage if you don’t yet know the shape of her life. To me, those are two very different things.

One thing that keeps happening in the quartet is that some characters protect other people by becoming unreadable to them. They don’t get the luxury of clean loyalty. They don’t get to stand in a doorway and explain themselves with tears in their eyes and earn immediate forgiveness. The world is too corrupted for that. The channels are too compromised. The bloodlines are too dangerous. Too many of these people are already marked, watched, and tangled up in things that were there long before them. So when protection shows up, it usually comes bent. Serafim is one of the earliest and sharpest embodiments of that pattern. She gives off that cheap spy vibe because she’s spent her life inside systems where sincerity gets you killed faster than deceit.

I have to be a little careful here because I don’t want this post turning into a spoiler map for Book Four. I’m not going to do that. Hall of Reflections does eventually wrap the whole early Dunya, Maria, Serafim, DATA ANGELS cluster into a much larger frame, and I’d rather let readers get there through the books than through me flattening it into lore notes. Still, I can say a little without giving too much away. Serafim’s behavior in Book One makes more sense once you understand that she’s not merely a rogue operator with a taste for cloak-and-dagger aesthetics. She’s a person shaped by buried family violence, by a twin relationship that matters far more than it seems at first, and by the lingering reach of Zakharovna in ways that go beyond simple political opposition. She’s not just fighting systems from outside. She’s carrying system damage inside herself.

So once you start seeing her that way, the cheap spy vibe feels less like style and more like a survival shell. It keeps people far enough away that they can’t romanticize her, but close enough that she can still move them where she needs them. Dunya is where that matters most.
